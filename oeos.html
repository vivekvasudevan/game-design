01

The Studio

Built from nothing. Run lean. For five years.

One Eyed Octopus Studios was a mobile game studio I co-founded in Pune in November 2013. We operated as an independent studio focused on original IP for mobile, building in a lean, fast-moving environment where everyone wore multiple hats and there was nowhere to hide from accountability.

There was no design team. No producer to hand off to. No creative director setting the vision above me. The product, the systems, the economy, the schedule, all of it was shared between co-founders, with my ownership sitting squarely in design, economy, and creative direction. That kind of environment builds a way of thinking you just can't fast-track in a bigger studio.

"The fastest way to understand every dimension of game design is to be responsible for all of them at once."

02

What I Did

Design, economy, production, all of it

Core Loop and Systems Design

Designed every core loop, mechanic, and player-facing system from the ground up across multiple projects over five years. This included defining daily engagement hooks, return triggers, and how early-session experience converted to long-term retention. With no inherited framework, everything was built from first principles and iterated against internal playtests and early user feedback.

Game Economy Design

Designed and balanced game economies end to end: currency sinks and sources, resource progression curves, monetisation scaffolding, and free-to-play pacing. Economy design at a startup, without analytics infrastructure to lean on, takes a particular discipline. You build conservative, test early, and tune aggressively. Across five years and several projects, I developed a strong gut sense for where an economy is broken well before the numbers confirm it.

GDD Authoring and Feature Documentation

Translated every design decision into documentation that engineering and art teams could build against. At a small studio, GDDs serve double duty: communicating intent to the team and acting as decision logs that keep everyone aligned when things change mid-development, which they always do. Learning to write GDDs that survive contact with production is one of the more underrated skills in game design.

Part-time Production

Alongside design, I ran production: sprint planning, milestone tracking, dependency management, and talking to people outside the team. Wearing both hats gave me a real feel for the tension between design ambition and production reality, and that makes me a more pragmatic, delivery-focused senior designer wherever I land.

UI and UX Design

With no dedicated UI designer on the team, I owned all the player-facing interface work: information architecture, HUD layout, menu flows, and iconography. This was the start of a design practice I kept developing through later product and UX roles. That background now gives me an unusually grounded sense of how UX principles actually apply to game UI, which matters more than most people admit.

03

What It Taught Me

Five years of compressed seniority

Running a studio means making decisions with incomplete information, real constraints, and genuine consequences. No safety net. The skills built here are foundational to how I approach any senior design role:

  • Scope instinct, knowing when a feature is worth building and when it's a production trap
  • Economy intuition, reading a game's numbers and knowing what's off before the data confirms it
  • Leadership without authority, getting a small team aligned when nobody formally reports to you
  • Pragmatic iteration, shipping something good rather than holding out forever for perfect
  • Cross-function fluency, understanding what engineering, art, and production each genuinely need from design
04

Why It Matters Now

Startup experience is a multiplier

When a studio says it wants a senior designer who can take ownership of a feature and drive it all the way to ship without constant direction from above, it's really describing someone with startup experience. At OEOS there was no one above me to catch a design decision that didn't hold up. Every system had to stand on its own.

That history is what lets me walk into a role as a senior designer, identify the highest-leverage design problems quickly, and get moving with minimal ramp-up time. The patterns are familiar even when the domain is new.
