01

The Game

India's first open-world third-person shooter

Mumbai Gullies was an ambitious PC title in the spirit of GTA. Open world, narrative driven, and heavy on gunplay, but rooted entirely in the culture, streets, and energy of Mumbai. We designed it to be played in full in both English and Hindi, with the same content and the same features in each. Nobody had really tried this in the Indian market before.

The game was in pre-production and systems design while I was on it. Because there was no existing reference for an Indian open-world shooter at this scale, every decision had to be reasoned out from first principles.

"This wasn't just a game set in India. It was built to feel like India, both for players who grew up in Mumbai and for players seeing it for the first time."

02

My Role

Sole design lead, reporting straight to the studio owner

I joined GameEon as a consultant, but in practice I was the game director for the project. I reported directly to Nikhil Malankar, the studio owner, which gave me real creative authority and, just as importantly, full accountability for how the design turned out.

There was no design team beneath me. I was the single source of design truth for a cross-functional team that spanned production, art, and story. Anything that touched game feel, systems, player flow, or how the narrative fit together came through me.

03

What I Designed

The foundational GDD and the core systems beneath it

The main thing I produced was the foundational Game Design Document, the source of truth the whole team would build against. But the GDD was really just the container. What I was actually creating were the systems that decided how the game felt to play at every level.

Image 05 · Document GDD page spread A page or two from the actual Game Design Document, like the vision page, a systems overview, or a feature spec. Blur or redact anything confidential. images/mumbai-gullies/05-gdd.png · 1600×900 · PNG (crisp text)

Foundational GDD

A thorough design document covering the vision, tone, core mechanics, player journey, systems overview, and feature specs. I wrote it to serve both creative direction and technical implementation, structured so engineers, artists, and producers could each find what they needed without wading through the rest.

Open world and traversal systems

I defined how the player moves through and interacts with Mumbai's open world: a third-person control scheme for keyboard, mouse, and gamepad, the feel of cover and gunplay, NPC behaviour triggers, environmental interactions, and the logic that keeps the city feeling alive rather than static. Traffic, crowds, and reactive street life were all part of that.

Image 06 · Diagram Open-world interaction / world-state diagram A FigJam or whiteboard systems map showing how player actions trigger NPC and world-state responses. A clean hand-drawn or Miro export works fine. images/mumbai-gullies/06-world-systems.png · 1600×1000 · PNG

Mission design framework

I created the structural framework for mission types, progression gates, and narrative hooks, and worked out the hierarchy between main story missions, side content, and ambient events. The goal was simple: always give players something meaningful to do, without overwhelming them or watering down the main story.

Image 07 · Diagram Mission structure and progression-gate flow A flowchart of main, side, and ambient missions, and how progression gates open up the map and the story. images/mumbai-gullies/07-mission-flow.png · 1600×1000 · PNG

Player progression and economy

I designed the economy from scratch: currency types, how resources flow, progression pacing, and reward structures. The trick was balancing a coherent progression loop against the realities of the Indian market, where player expectations and spending habits look quite different from Western markets.

Image 08 · Diagram / Sheet Economy and progression flow A sources-and-sinks diagram for currencies and resources, or a screenshot of the Google Sheets balance model. images/mumbai-gullies/08-economy.png · 1600×1000 · PNG

Bilingual design, with English and Hindi at equal weight

I designed the game for genuine bilingual delivery, not a translation layer bolted onto an English product. That meant decisions across UI layout (so text could expand and contract for each script), voice line integration, dialogue framing that actually lands culturally, and handling content that simply doesn't carry the same meaning across both languages. Both versions were treated as the real thing.

04

Leading Across Teams

Design as the connective tissue between disciplines

On a project this size, the game director's job is less about generating ideas and more about keeping everyone building toward the same picture. I worked closely with production, art, and story every day to surface blockers early, settle conflicts between disciplines, and keep design decisions in step with what each team actually needed to move forward.

  • Production: flagging design decisions with time or resource implications early, keeping scope clear, and talking through trade-offs before they became problems.
  • Art: turning design intent into concrete visual briefs, reviewing environments against systems needs, and giving feedback on world layout from a gameplay point of view.
  • Story: keeping narrative systems and mission writing consistent with the design framework and progression logic, and coordinating content pacing across both language versions.
